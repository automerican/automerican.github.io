Is Tesla the Future of Electric Cars?

By Jordyn HamiltonApril 26, 2020

Elon Musk, Brake or Bust?

Just about every folk in America and abroad knows about the well-spoken, anime loving, meme lord Elon-chan, but how does his car brand represent the future of the automotive industry and can they claim a top spot in the future of electric vehicles? 

From the bottom to the top Tesla has been riding the EV wave to the leaderboard in the past few years with their unmatched pace for innovation in the electric market. With the relatively recent release of the Tesla Cybertruck, and subsequent stock price jump, it really seemed like Tesla could ride this wave forever. However, it might surprise many to learn that Tesla isn’t too good at doing what companies are generally made to do- generating profit.

Tesla Profit Margins

Now, the fact the Tesla sells the most popular electric vehicles of all time is irrefutable. Despite the significantly large time gap between the Nissan Leaf (the second most popular electric vehicle), which was released in December 2010 and the release date of the Tesla Model 3 in September of 2017, the Model 3 has managed to not only catch up to but surpass the Leaf total global sales. Even if you narrow it down to just the North American market, Tesla’s Model 3 still comes out on top over the Model S, Chevy Volt, and Nissan Leaf once more. So shouldn’t they be making bank?

Tesla started 2019 off with major cost-cutting measures to keep the company from going under within the year and missed their estimated revenue in the first quarter by about $650million. The second quarter wasn’t so forgiving either, netting Tesla another $408million loss in revenue. By the end of 2019, Tesla was still unable to turn a profit, with a total loss of $862million by the end of the year.

According to Donna Hull and Tom Randall of Automotive News, one of the main culprits seems to be Tesla’s pioneering “direct-to-consumer sales” system which bypasses third-party dealerships, opting for Tesla-run shops where consumers can only buy directly from Tesla itself. On paper it seems like an amazing idea; no dealerships marking up the MSRP prices, no sly-tongued salesmen making commissions selling their souls to sell you the most expensive model, and more salary-based jobs within the Tesla company. Unfortunately, it’s not all sunshine and rainbows as these Tesla-operated stores are apparently cutting deep into Musk’s pockets, forcing the closure of many across the country.

The $35k Promise

Probably the single biggest issue at Tesla right now is the confusion with the Tesla Model 3 Standard Range version. Back in March of 2016, they unveiled the Model 3 with a starting price of $35,000USD. However, as most know now, it actually released with a base price of around $40,000. Early in 2019, Tesla did finally release their $35,000 model, three years after promised, but to this day there remains a lack of clarity in purchasing this version of the Model 3 as it isn’t directly purchasable on Tesla’s website. Car Confections covers the detail more in-depth in this video.

Competitors

While Tesla has admittedly made leaps and bounds in terms of EV technology, its established competitors haven’t been idly standing by to enjoy the show. Following the Dieselgate scandal we mentioned in our article on diesel cars here, Volkswagen decided to swear off diesel and gas-powered vehicles to pursue the electric market in hopes of re-branding as an environmentally friendly brand. Many other brands have also recently dipped into the electric market.

Companies like Nissan and Chevy began experimenting with electric consumer vehicles years ago, but as more and more companies hop on the bandwagon the element of competition is starting to fill the air. It seems like every couple of months there’s a new EV announced. A new Chevy Volt here, a VW E-Golf there, here have a Rivian electric pickup truck! With all these new introductions to the market, it’s hard to tell who might come out on top, but we’re fairly sure it won’t be Tesla.
